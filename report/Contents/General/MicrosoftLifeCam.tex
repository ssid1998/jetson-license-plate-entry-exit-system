\chapter{Microsoft LifeCam}
\label{chap:lifecam}


\section{Introduction}

The Microsoft LifeCam Show serves as a compact \ac{USB}-based web camera that functions as the primary sensor for image acquisition in this project. 
It is able to captures video frames and subsequently transfer them to the NVIDIA Jetson Nano enabling license plate detection on a real time basis. 
The camera provides a maximum digital resolution of 800$\times$600 pixels, integrates a 2~MP \ac{CMOS} sensor, and supports automatic focus adjustment and built-in microphone audio input \cite{microsoft_lifecam_datasheet}. 
Owing to its compact design and native \ac{USB}~2.0 connectivity, it is suited to embedded vision tasks where plug-and-play functionality and frame rate stability is important \cite{techradar_lifecam_review}.

\section{General}
The LifeCam Show functions as a \textbf{\ac{USB} Video Class (UVC)} device compliant with the open \ac{USB} standard for web cameras and requires no proprietary drivers on modern Linux distributions \cite{linux_uvc_driver}. 
On connecting with the Jetson Nano interface, it functions automatically as \texttt{/dev/video0}, made accessible via libraries like OpenCV or GStreamer.  
Stable compatibility is ensured across Windows, Linux, and JetPack environments \cite{jetson_nano_doc}.

The camera uses a fixed-lens optical system with automatic focus control and is capable of real-time digital zooming up to 5×. 
In the context of this project, the camera is mounted on a stand that is adjustable, and is positioned such that the height between the camera and vehicle license plates is approximately 1.5~m, thus aiding in clear image resolution within a range of  0.5–1.5~m.

\section{Hardware Configuration}
\label{sec:lifecam_specific}

The model specific to this project is the \textbf{Microsoft LifeCam Show (Model~1139)}and connects with the Jetson Nano via a \ac{USB}~2.0 Type-A interface, capable of supplying both \textbf{data transmission} and \textbf{5~V~DC power} (up to 500~mA).  
While operational, the LifeCam functions as a plug-and-play UVC device, which Linux-based systems such as JetPack~5.x are able to automatically recognize, appearing under \texttt{/dev/video0} \cite{linux_uvc_driver, jetson_nano_doc}.  

\bigskip
\textbf{Design Specifics and Mounting Accessory Description:}  
The LifeCam Show comprises of a vertical body encased in silver and black housing, optimized towards portable and fixed installations.  
As demonstrated by Figure~\ref{fig:lifecam_assembly}, the camera is equipped with accessories that allow for flexible deployments in varying setups. 
Included are the \textbf{attachment disc}, \textbf{laptop clip}, \textbf{desktop stand}, and a \textbf{carrying case}. These accessories serve an individual mounting purpose, described as follows:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Attachment Disc:} A metal based magnetic disc that allows the camera to attach with flat metallic surfaces, similar to monitor frames or tripod mounting stands.
	\item \textbf{Laptop Clip:} A detachable clip mechanism allowing the camera to be mounted, providing stable positioning for image captures.
	\item \textbf{Desktop Stand:} A height-adjustable stand designed for tabletop or field deployment, to position the camera at a height of approximately 1.5~m and allow it to be tilted at an angle of 25° in order to read license plates.
	\item \textbf{Carrying Case:} Provides an enclosure for safe transport and storage preventing dust accumulation or lens damage.
\end{itemize}

The modularity of design makes the LifeCam Show suited for laboratory and field application tasks.  
In the context of this project, the \textbf{desktop stand} configuration was selected to maintain a fixed, vibration-free viewing position while ensuring consistent alignment of license plates under various lighting conditions.

\section{Data Characteristics}
\label{sec:lifecam_data}

\subsection{Data Types}
The Microsoft LifeCam Show captures \textbf{RGB color video frames} at a maximum resolution of 800$\times$600~pixels (VGA), encoded as 8-bit unsigned integer values per color channel (24-bit color depth).  
Each frame consists of three color planes — Blue, Green, and Red (BGR order) — compatible with OpenCV’s \texttt{cv::Mat} format \cite{opencv_doc_2024}.  
In addition to video, the built-in mono microphone provides a \textbf{Pulse Code Modulated (PCM)} audio stream at 16-bit depth and 16~kHz sampling rate \cite{microsoft_lifecam_datasheet}.  
Both data streams comply with the \ac{USB}~Video~Class (UVC~1.1) standard \cite{linux_uvc_driver}.

\subsection{Data Structure}
Captured video frames are stored in sequential frame buffers in system memory using the \texttt{v4l2} (Video4Linux2) driver layer.  
Each frame corresponds to a three dimensional matrix of dimensions $(H, W, C)$ = (600, 800, 3) and corresponding datatype \texttt{uint8}.  

In order to synchronize with inference pipelines, timestamps are attached. This ensures chronological integrity. 
As a part of Jetson Nano pipeline, such buffers are passed directly to GPU memory in order to be preprocessed via \textbf{Zero-Copy DMA} transfer, leading to a minimization of CPU overhead \cite{jetson_nano_doc}.  

\subsection{Data Quantity}
At 30~frames~per~second and VGA resolution, the raw uncompressed video throughput is approximately:
\[
800 \times 600 \times 3 \times 30 = 43.2~\text{MB/s}.
\]
When encoded using MJPEG compression, the effective bandwidth reduces to 4–6~MB/s, depending on scene complexity.  
An hour of recording requires roughly 1.5–2.0~GB of storage in MJPEG format.  
This data rate is well within the \ac{USB}~2.0 specification’s maximum of 480~Mbps, allowing for stable streaming without frame drops \cite{linux_uvc_driver}.  

\subsection{Data Quality}
The LifeCam Show is able to consistently deliver sharp images under standard indoor lighting conditions (200–800~lux).  
Its \ac{CMOS} sensor is capable of delivering medium low-light sensitivity, although exhibiting visible noise under 100~lux illumination levels.  
Color reproduction fidelity is high (average $\Delta E$ <~6.0) under balanced daylight LED lighting \cite{techradar_lifecam_rev	iew}.  
The auto-focus system stabilizes focus for object distances between 0.5–1.5~m, optimal for fixed-mount license plate imaging.
The camera is limited in terms of manual gain control, white balance locking, and lens calibration data, causing slight variability under dynamic lighting \cite{microsoft_lifecam_datasheet}.

\section{Constraints}
\label{sec:lifecam_constraints}

There are multiple operational and environmental limitations with respect to embedded AI deployment using the Microsoft LifeCam described as under:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Power Supply:} 5~V~DC at 500~mA from a \ac{USB}~2.0 port.  
	
	A longer cable than 2m might lead to under-voltage and unstable frame captures. 
	
	\item \textbf{Bandwidth:} A \ac{USB}~2.0 interface (480~Mbps shared) limitations.  
	High-speed devices (e.g., SSD or CSI camera) concurrently used may result in reduced throughput.
	
	\item \textbf{Dependency on Lighting Conditions:}  
	Detection performance tends to degrade within low-lighting environments (<100~lux), also influenced by overexposure (>2000~lux).  
	
	As a recommendation, the use of additional lighting is suggested. This promotes optimal license plate recognition given consistent lighting conditions.
	
	\item \textbf{Temperature Range:}  
	Ambient temperatures ranging from 0–40~°C promote stability in operation.  
	Temperatures exceeding 45~°C may result in the introduction of electronic noise and focus drift.
	
	\item \textbf{Mounting Stability:}  
	
	In order to prevent blurred image frames during capture, the camera is to be fixed securely and firmly. 
	
	Vibrations exceeding 10~Hz may lead to misaligned focus leading to license plate detection inaccuracies.
	
	\item \textbf{Constraints with System Integration:}  
	Connections via \ac{USB} hub should ensure ample power delivery. It is preferable to directy use the Jetson Nano port (J4).
\end{itemize}

Although constraints exist, the LifeCam Show is able to produce a consistent video quality feed for real-time embedded vision tasks, positioning it as a good alternative to complex and expensive industrial cameras for AI inference \cite{edgeAIbook,techradar_lifecam_review}.

\section{Calibration}
Unlike analog cameras or industrial sensors, the LifeCam Show uses a factory-calibrated auto-focus system.  
However, lens calibration for intrinsic and extrinsic parameters (e.g., for perspective correction) can be performed using OpenCV’s camera calibration module \cite{opencv_doc_2024}.  
Calibration is optional for this project, as the camera is used in a fixed-mount setup.

\section{Simple Application}
The LifeCam feed is processed by the YOLOv8-based license plate detector implemented on the Jetson Nano.  
Each frame is preprocessed (cropped, resized, normalized) and passed to the neural network for real-time inference.  
This enables localized object detection without dependence on cloud connectivity \cite{edgeAIbook}.
