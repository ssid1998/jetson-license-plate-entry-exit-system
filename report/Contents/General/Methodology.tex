\section{Introduction}
\index{Methodology!Introduction}

\noindent Automatic License Plate Recognition (ALPR) systems have become essential components of modern intelligent transportation systems, parking management, and access control applications \cite{du2013automatic,silva2018license}. These systems require accurate detection and recognition of vehicle license plates under diverse environmental conditions, including variations in lighting, viewing angles, occlusion, and plate design. Deploying ALPR systems on edge devices such as the NVIDIA Jetson Nano introduces additional constraints related to computational resources, power consumption, and real-time performance \cite{shi2016edge,nvidia_jetson}.  

\noindent The methodology adopted in this project integrates three complementary components: the \textit{Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining (CRISP-DM)}, the \textit{Knowledge Discovery in Databases (KDD) process}, and a modular machine learning pipeline. CRISP-DM provides a structured, iterative framework for managing the project lifecycle, ensuring alignment between business objectives—such as reliable license plate detection, timestamp recording, and fee calculation—and technical implementation \cite{chapman2000crisp}. By guiding the project through phases of business understanding, data preparation, modeling, evaluation, and deployment, CRISP-DM ensures that each step is systematically planned and optimized, which is particularly critical when operating under the resource constraints of embedded hardware.  

\noindent The KDD process complements CRISP-DM by emphasizing the systematic transformation of raw data into actionable knowledge \cite{fayyad1996knowledge}. In this project, KDD is applied to both the offline training phase and the real-time deployment phase. During training, labeled images are preprocessed, augmented, and transformed into structured input-output pairs suitable for supervised learning. During deployment, the process is applied to live camera frames, converting raw pixels into bounding boxes via YOLO and subsequently into alphanumeric characters using EasyOCR \cite{jaidedai2020easyocr}. This separation ensures computational efficiency and maintains consistency between training and operational data distributions, which is essential for reliable inference on the Jetson Nano \cite{shi2016edge}.  

\noindent The machine learning pipeline operationalizes these methodologies by implementing the core computational workflow for license plate detection and recognition. YOLO was chosen as the object detection model due to its single-pass architecture, which balances high accuracy with low inference latency \cite{redmon2016you,bochkovskiy2020yolov4}. EasyOCR is used for character recognition, providing modularity and robustness to variations in plate fonts, orientation, and illumination \cite{jaidedai2020easyocr}. Preprocessing steps such as resizing, normalization, and data augmentation further enhance generalization and maintain consistency between the training dataset and live input frames \cite{shorten2019survey}. Detected plates and corresponding entry and exit timestamps are stored in a lightweight SQLite database, enabling subsequent computation of parking duration and fees without reliance on cloud infrastructure \cite{sqlite_doc}.  

\noindent By integrating CRISP-DM, the KDD process, and a carefully designed ML pipeline, this methodology addresses the dual challenges of \textit{accuracy} and \textit{real-time performance} on a constrained edge device. It provides a structured framework for dataset management, model training, deployment, and knowledge extraction, while ensuring that operational outputs—license plate numbers and timestamps—are reliable, interpretable, and actionable. This integrated approach demonstrates how modern ALPR systems can be effectively implemented on embedded hardware, paving the way for efficient, low-latency, and autonomous edge computing applications.  

\section{KDD Process}
\index{Methodology!KDD}

\section{Methodology: Knowledge Discovery in Databases (KDD)}

\noindent
The Knowledge Discovery in Databases (KDD) process provides a structured framework for transforming raw data into meaningful and actionable knowledge through a sequence of well-defined stages \cite{fayyad1996knowledge}. In this project, KDD is applied holistically across both the \textit{model training phase} and the \textit{operational deployment phase}. Unlike methodologies that focus solely on post-deployment analytics, this consolidated KDD approach captures how knowledge is progressively extracted from raw visual data, beginning with dataset construction and model learning, and culminating in real-time license plate analysis and fee computation on embedded hardware.

\subsection{Data Selection}

The KDD process begins with data selection, where relevant data sources are identified and collected. For the training phase, a dataset consisting of 1,243 labeled images containing German vehicle license plates was selected. These images represent diverse environmental conditions, including variations in illumination, plate orientation, distance, and background complexity. Such diversity is critical for learning robust visual patterns in automatic license plate recognition (ALPR) systems \cite{du2013automatic,silva2018license}.

During deployment, data selection occurs dynamically, as image frames are captured in real time from an external camera connected to the NVIDIA Jetson Nano. Only frames containing vehicles or candidate license plates are forwarded to subsequent stages, ensuring computational efficiency and relevance of the extracted data.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing and Cleaning}

In both training and deployment stages, preprocessing and cleaning play a central role in improving data quality. During training, images are loaded using the OpenCV library and undergo resizing, normalization, and color space conversion to conform to the input requirements of the YOLO-based detection model \cite{bradski2000opencv,opencv_wiki}. Noisy or low-quality samples are either corrected or excluded to prevent degradation of the learning process.

To enhance generalization and mitigate overfitting, data augmentation techniques such as geometric transformations and brightness variation are applied during training \cite{shorten2019survey}. During deployment, preprocessing ensures consistency between live camera input and the data distribution observed during training, which is essential for maintaining reliable inference performance on embedded platforms.

\subsection{Data Transformation}

The transformation stage converts preprocessed data into representations suitable for knowledge extraction. During training, annotated images are transformed into structured input-output pairs consisting of pixel data and corresponding bounding box labels. These representations enable supervised learning of spatial and semantic patterns associated with license plates \cite{redmon2016you}.

In the deployment phase, transformation occurs through inference. Raw image frames are converted into bounding box predictions by the trained YOLO model, effectively transforming unstructured pixel data into structured object-level information \cite{bochkovskiy2020yolov4}. Detected license plate regions are further transformed into alphanumeric text using optical character recognition, bridging the gap between visual perception and symbolic data representation \cite{jaidedai2020easyocr}.

\subsection{Data Mining}

Data mining represents the core analytical stage of the KDD process, where algorithms extract patterns or models from transformed data \cite{han2011data}. In the training phase, data mining is realized through deep learning model optimization. The YOLO detection model learns hierarchical visual features that distinguish license plates from background elements by iteratively minimizing a loss function over the training dataset.

This learning process embodies pattern discovery at scale, as the model encodes complex spatial and contextual relationships within its parameters \cite{lecun2015deep}. In the deployment phase, the trained models continue to perform data mining in an online manner, applying learned patterns to incoming image streams to detect license plates and recognize characters in real time.

\subsection{Interpretation and Evaluation}

Interpretation and evaluation occur at multiple stages of the KDD pipeline. During training, model performance is evaluated using standard object detection and recognition metrics, such as precision, recall, and qualitative inspection of predicted bounding boxes \cite{everingham2010pascal}. These evaluations guide hyperparameter tuning and model selection, ensuring that the discovered patterns generalize beyond the training dataset.

During deployment, interpretation focuses on the correctness and consistency of extracted license plate numbers and timestamps. Detection errors, OCR inaccuracies, and duplicate records are analyzed and filtered where necessary to maintain data integrity. System-level performance metrics, including inference latency and reliability on the Jetson Nano, are also evaluated to ensure operational feasibility.

\subsection{Knowledge Presentation and Storage}

The final stage of the KDD process involves presenting and storing extracted knowledge in a form suitable for practical use. In the deployment phase, recognized license plate numbers are paired with entry and exit timestamps and stored in a SQLite database. SQLite is well suited for this task due to its lightweight architecture, transactional support, and compatibility with embedded systems \cite{sqlite_doc}.

Higher-level knowledge is subsequently derived by computing temporal differences between entry and exit events and applying predefined pricing rules to calculate parking fees. This transformation of low-level visual observations into actionable financial information represents the culmination of the KDD process, enabling autonomous decision-making at the edge without reliance on cloud-based infrastructure \cite{shi2016edge}.

\noindent
By consolidating both the training and operational stages within the KDD framework, this methodology demonstrates how knowledge discovery extends beyond post-deployment analytics and encompasses the entire lifecycle of a data-driven computer vision system.

\section{Methodology Justification}
\index{Methodology!Justification}

\noindent Each methodological choice has been critically selected to address the constraints of real-time edge deployment, limited dataset size, and variability in visual conditions.  

\begin{itemize}  
	\item Dataset selection:\\
	A dataset of 1,243 labeled images was chosen to balance the need for sufficient variability with the practical constraints of data collection and labeling. This dataset provides coverage of diverse lighting, viewing angles, and occlusion scenarios, which is critical for robust license plate detection \cite{silva2018license}. While a larger dataset could improve model generalization, it would increase labeling effort, storage requirements, and training time, which are non-trivial considerations for this project.  
	
	\item Separation of training and deployment:\\
	The KDD process was split into distinct training and deployment stages to optimize computational efficiency. Training occurs offline on a workstation with GPU acceleration, allowing iterative model optimization and hyperparameter tuning. Deployment operates online on the Jetson Nano, performing real-time inference. This separation prevents resource overload and ensures that real-time constraints are satisfied \cite{shi2016edge}.  
	
	\item Structured data transformation:\\
	By converting raw images into bounding boxes and subsequently into textual license plate data via OCR, the KDD process ensures that live input frames are consistently processed in the same manner as the training data. This consistency is crucial to maintain accuracy and reliability when the system is deployed in real-world conditions \cite{du2013automatic,jaidedai2020easyocr}.  
\end{itemize}  

\subsection{Justification for the Machine Learning Pipeline}  
\noindent The machine learning pipeline was designed with both accuracy and computational feasibility in mind:  

\begin{itemize}  
	\item YOLO for license plate detection:\\
	YOLO was selected for its single-pass detection capability, which enables fast inference necessary for real-time operation \cite{redmon2016you,bochkovskiy2020yolov4}. Alternative CNN-based detection models such as Faster R-CNN or SSD were considered but rejected due to their higher computational and memory requirements, which would compromise performance on the Jetson Nano.  
	
	\item EasyOCR for character recognition:\\
	Using EasyOCR as a separate recognition module allows for independent optimization of detection and recognition. This modularity is advantageous for edge deployment, as it reduces GPU load and increases system flexibility. EasyOCR also handles the variability in license plate fonts, orientations, and lighting conditions more effectively than a monolithic end-to-end CNN model \cite{jaidedai2020easyocr}.  
	
	\item Preprocessing and augmentation:\\
	Images were resized, normalized, and augmented to simulate real-world variations. These steps mitigate overfitting due to the relatively small dataset and ensure that the model generalizes to different environmental conditions, which is particularly important for reliable edge inference \cite{shorten2019survey}.  
	
	\item Dataset size and augmentation trade-off:\\
	The chosen dataset size, combined with augmentation, provides sufficient diversity for training without incurring the high computational costs of very large datasets. This balance allows the model to achieve high detection and recognition accuracy while remaining feasible for deployment on the Jetson Nano.  
\end{itemize}  

\subsection{Reflective Evaluation}  
\noindent The combination of the KDD process and a modular machine learning pipeline provides a systematic and efficient approach to license plate detection on an embedded device. By structuring data selection, preprocessing, transformation, and model deployment, the methodology ensures:  

\begin{itemize}  
	\item Consistency and reliability in converting raw visual data into actionable knowledge (license plate numbers and timestamps).  
	\item Real-time performance on the Jetson Nano without overloading hardware resources.  
	\item Flexibility for future updates or retraining, as each component (detection, recognition, storage) can be modified independently.  
\end{itemize}  

\noindent Overall, this methodology balances accuracy, efficiency, and edge-device feasibility, making it well-suited for real-world license plate detection, timestamp recording, and subsequent fee calculation.
